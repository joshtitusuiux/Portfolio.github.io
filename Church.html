Increasing Website Traffic For Cross Roads Church

Overview:
I was hired as an intern to conduct UX research and
design a new website for Cross Roads, a 280+ member church. I leveraged Google Analytics 4 to assess the
website performance.


  • View the Website Here


Timeline:
August 21, 2023 - January 25, 2024.
My Role:
UX Designer, UX Researcher, HTML Coder, Web Designer
Tools:
Google Analytics 4, HTML, Moderated User Testing, Google Surveys, Clover, Powerpoint















  • 51.82% increase in returning website visitors compared to the previous website.

  • 57.68% increase in Google Analytics 4 engaged user sessions (sessions that lasted longer than 10 seconds and include a key event or multiple page views) compared to the previous website.

  • 61.53% increase in website total new users and 5.38% bounce rate decrease compared to the previous website.



The Problem:
How do I create a website that stays on brand with the company and increases digital metrics and human-connection to Cross Roads church?
The Solution:
I used best-practice design principles and HTML5 to code a solution with the help of the tool Clover that resulted in multiple improved metrics and positive testimonials.

Initial User Research:
I executed 26 moderated testing sessions over zoom and a seven question survey with participants within my target demographic (non-church members aged 18-45) to test the user-friendliness and experience of the new website to gain insights into improvement.


Participants were asked to complete tasks on the website and quantify their experience and level of difficulty completing tasks on the website from easy, difficult, or hard. Participants were asked to describe their thought process as they used the website and give their likes and dislikes.






1. Task 1: Find the Livestream.
2. Task 2: Find the donation form.
3. Task 3: Find the events lists.
4. Task 4: Find the welcome team sign up form.









Insights:
The study revealed that my website redesign was
easy to use and had zero-to-no painpoints for
almost all participants. There was positive feedback
that the hamburger menu was slightly tedious for participants to become aware of on mobile version,
that the live stream could be a bit easier to locate,
and that some pictures needed to be slightly
adjusted to be lined up to the edge of the page. It
was through user testing that these insights were
possible and improvements were made.










Takeaways:
As my first solo paid service for a client, I found
metrics and testimonials reflected the end result
improved the human connection to the organ-
ization I created the product for. This was my
first experience not working in a group of other
designers, and because of that it took longer to
complete the product than I am used to. I enjoy collaborating with other team-members as opposed
to working alone. In future Sprints, I would spend
more time doing contextual inquiry research, and
interviews as opposed to only moderated user
testing (although moderated testing is undoubtedly
among the most effective forms of user research),
as diverse strategeies for user research may turn up
more unique actionable insights. I feel that as my
first solo project, I am thoroughly ecstatic at the
final website design and impact on the organization
I designed it for.
